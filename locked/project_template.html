Let's start with

The kids were 17, 13, 12, 9, 7, and 3. They checked in late the night before after mom was taken into custody. Kay, the social worker, had just been assigned to the case and spent the morning reviewing the files, making calls, leaving messages, and sending emails. It became apparent that it's unlikely to find anyone in the family to care for them, nor any family friends. Kay's next hope was to find a long term place, ideally a foster family that can take all 6 of them. She knew that would also be unlikely given the shortage of the resource families and facilities. So she went to the emergency foster home to meet the kids for the first time. She had to ask them a question:

If you are to be split, which siblings you want to be with?

It turned out all younger kids wanted to stay with the big sis except one, the 12 years old. Holding up his head and fighting back his tears, the boy said he's old enough, he could stay with the 9 years old and look after him.

A new system

Child Welfare Digital Services (CWDS) is a collaboration of California State and local government agencies that support their shared stakeholders through technology to assure the safety, permanency and well-being of children at risk of abuse, neglect or exploitation. They have 2 jobs:

  • maintaining and operating the existing Child Welfare Services / Case Management System (CWS/CMS)
  • developing the Child Welfare Services – California Automated Response and Engagement System (CWS-CARES), so it can replace CWS/CMS

The new system is expected to respond to users’ needs while maintaining the best standards for security and data integrity to aid child welfare professionals in the vital assistance, oversight and case management of our most vulnerable populations.

Why it is so hard

To say it out loud, child welfare systems aross the nation are broken. 424,000 children are in foster care in any given day nationwide. In 2019, over 672,000 children spent time in U.S. foster care. [Source]

While there're more children entering the foster care every year, there're fewer families and facilities are available for them. The most significant challenges state child welfare agencies have been facing are [Source]:

  • Providing an adequate level of services for children and families,
  • Recruiting and retaining caseworkers, and
  • Finding appropriate homes for children

These challenges are reflected throughout our research and discovery. As a result of these challenges, many foster children can't get placed to more appropriate homes, or can't get to be placed in time. Many of them have to go through different homes/placements. One of our interviewees, a 15 years old foster child from California Youth Connection (CYC), has been placed 53 times.

If it takes a village to raise a child, how much it takes to raise the half million children in US child welfare system?

In reality though

We skipped the discovery & framing phase which would have allowed us to look at the problem space from end to end. The entire project was divided into 7 modules based on the programs and domains, each module had its own team consists of design, product, and engineering folks from multiple vendors, subject matter experts, product owners, and service managers from the state or counties. There were about 20 - 30 some people in each module/service team. The plan was to onboard one team at a time, about 2-3 teams per year. The onboarded teams were expected to deliver "working codes" quickly, therefore the researches were very module and solution driven early on.

However ...

However a year into the project started to feel the pain of solving problems within domain silos. For example, the state released a Resource Family Approval directive, but counties had their own ways to approve foster family for decades. They had developed systems and programs accordingly, which create obsticle for say counties to share resources and data, or collabrating across services. During interviews, large counties with thousands of employees, small ones with a handful of employees, different programs (Intakes, Assesment, Approval, Case Management, etc.) were always asking something different, something tailored for their special needs. We had no choice but demolishing the silos and looking at the services at a whole, rather than the sum of their parts.
